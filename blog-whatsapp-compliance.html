WhatsApp in your contact centre: what compliance actually requires

9 Oct 2025 · 5 min read · Kaptea Team
The short answer

Adding WhatsApp to your contact centre creates real regulatory obligations: you must capture every message, archive it for defined retention periods, make conversations available for monitoring and review, and ensure data is processed in compliance with GDPR and any sector-specific rules. Most organisations using WhatsApp for customer service have not yet addressed all of these requirements.

  2. The regulatory landscape

WhatsApp has 2 billion active users worldwide. In Ireland and the UK, it is often the first messaging application people reach for, ahead of SMS. For contact centres looking to meet customers where they actually communicate, adding WhatsApp feels like an obvious move.

And it is. But the way most organisations bolt WhatsApp onto their contact centre infrastructure introduces compliance exposure that is easy to miss and difficult to remediate retrospectively.

This article is for compliance leads, operations directors, and technology teams at regulated organisations who are evaluating WhatsApp as a customer communication channel. It is also relevant to anyone who has already deployed WhatsApp and wants to understand what they may have missed.

Why WhatsApp is different from your other channels

Voice calls have been subject to compliance recording requirements in financial services for decades. Email archiving is well understood. SMS logging is a solved problem for any mature contact centre platform.

WhatsApp sits in a different category in the minds of many compliance teams, because it started as a consumer application. The assumption is that it operates like an informal channel. The regulatory reality is that as soon as you use it to conduct regulated business, it becomes a regulated communication channel with all the obligations that entails.

The additional complexity is that WhatsApp's native end-to-end encryption creates technical barriers to the kinds of monitoring and archiving that regulators require. Using the WhatsApp Business App (the free application for small businesses) makes compliance almost impossible at scale. The WhatsApp Business API, accessed through an approved provider such as Twilio, is the only viable route for organisations with genuine compliance obligations.

The regulatory landscape

The obligations vary by sector, but the pattern is consistent across regulated industries in Ireland, the UK, and the EU.

Financial services (FCA-regulated firms, CBI-regulated firms): MiFID II requires that communications relating to client orders, transactions, and related advice are recorded and retained for a minimum of five years. The FCA has made clear that this extends to all electronic communication channels used for regulated business, including WhatsApp. Several major financial institutions have faced enforcement action and material fines specifically for WhatsApp communications that were not captured.

Healthcare: Communications containing or referencing patient information are subject to data protection requirements under GDPR and, in Ireland, the Health (Assisted Human Reproduction) Act framework. The principle is that patient communications must be securely handled, stored in auditable systems, and accessible only to authorised personnel.

Utilities and energy: Consumer-facing communications, including complaints and billing disputes, may be subject to retention and retrieval requirements under sector-specific regulation from the CRU (Ireland) or Ofgem (UK).

Enforcement precedent

In 2022, US regulators imposed over $1.8 billion in fines on major financial institutions for failures to preserve employee WhatsApp communications. European regulators have followed a similar trajectory. The message is clear: "we didn't know we needed to capture those" is not a defence.

Archiving and retrieval obligations

The core requirement, across virtually all regulated sectors, is that business communications must be captured, stored in a tamper-evident format, and retrievable on request by regulators or in response to legal proceedings.

For WhatsApp, this means:

  • Every inbound and outbound message must be captured in full, including any attachments, images, or documents exchanged.
  • Metadata must be preserved: sender identity, recipient, timestamp, and channel.
  • Storage must be in a system that prevents tampering with or deletion of records during the retention period.
  • Search and retrieval must be possible within the timeframes regulators specify (typically 72 hours for urgent requests).

None of this is possible with a standard WhatsApp Business App deployment. It requires an enterprise API integration with a compliant archiving solution, built into your contact centre infrastructure from the outset.

Monitoring and supervision requirements

In financial services, compliance monitoring of communications is an active obligation. Supervisors must be able to review a statistically meaningful sample of customer communications on an ongoing basis. For some firms, this extends to real-time or near-real-time supervisory access.

WhatsApp's end-to-end encryption protects messages between the consumer's phone and WhatsApp's servers. But when accessed via the Business API, messages pass through the API in unencrypted form before being delivered to the business. This is the point at which compliant platforms capture, archive, and make available for supervision the content of every conversation.

The implication: your contact centre infrastructure must be built to intercept and process WhatsApp messages at the API layer, not after the fact.

GDPR and data residency considerations

WhatsApp is owned by Meta. Under GDPR, transmitting personal data to a third party located outside the EEA requires either an adequacy decision, standard contractual clauses, or another approved transfer mechanism. The legal basis for processing the personal data contained in WhatsApp conversations must be established and documented.

For organisations operating under Irish or UK data protection law, the practical requirements include:

  • A Data Processing Agreement (DPA) with your WhatsApp Business API provider.
  • Clear documentation of the legal basis for processing personal data via WhatsApp (typically contract performance or legitimate interests).
  • Data residency controls for archived communications, particularly if your sector requires data to be stored within the EEA.
  • Customer consent mechanisms for using WhatsApp, particularly for outbound communications.

How to approach WhatsApp compliance properly

The organisations that get WhatsApp compliance right do not retrofit it. They design it into the architecture from the beginning, with archiving, monitoring, and access controls built at the infrastructure layer.

The starting point is the WhatsApp Business API via an approved provider, not the consumer app or WhatsApp Business App. From there, the integration must be built to capture every message, route it through compliant archiving, and surface it to supervisory tools with the right access controls.

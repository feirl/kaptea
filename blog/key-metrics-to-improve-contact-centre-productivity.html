Key Metrics to Improve Contact Centre Productivity in 2026

Alan McNab · 9 June 2026 · 7 min read
The short answer

Contact centre productivity metrics are the operational measurements that reveal whether a contact centre is resolving customer issues efficiently and sustainably. The eight that matter most: first call resolution, average handle time, occupancy rate, abandonment rate, CSAT or CES, cost per contact, schedule adherence, and agent utilisation. The trap most contact centres fall into is optimising these metrics in isolation: improving one at the expense of another creates problems faster than it solves them.

Why the right metrics change what you optimise for

Contact centre productivity is the output of a system with competing pressures: customer expectations, agent capacity, cost constraints, and compliance requirements. The metrics you track determine which pressures your operation actually responds to.

A contact centre that tracks only Average Handle Time will find agents cutting calls short. One that tracks only CSAT will find agents spending 40 minutes on interactions that could be resolved in eight. Neither is a productive operation. The discipline is choosing a metric set that creates aligned incentives across efficiency, quality, and sustainability.

In regulated industries, there is an additional layer: some metrics have compliance implications. An abandonment rate above a certain threshold in a financial services context can attract regulatory attention. A contact centre that has no reliable measurement of repeat contacts may be unable to demonstrate complaint resolution under FCA oversight rules.

The eight metrics that drive productivity

1. First Call Resolution (FCR)

Target: 75%+

The percentage of contacts fully resolved on the first interaction without a repeat contact within 7 days. FCR is the single most powerful productivity lever because it determines how much of your inbound volume is necessary. A 70% FCR rate means 30% of contacts are, by definition, repeat contacts from previous failures. Eliminating each percentage point of unnecessary re-contact volume reduces total handled volume without touching staffing.

Watch for: FCR measured by agent self-report or call disposition codes is not reliable. Measure by tracking actual repeat contacts from the same customer within 7 days about the same issue type.

2. Average Handle Time (AHT)

Varies by channel and sector

The average duration of a customer interaction, including talk time, hold time, and after-call work. AHT is useful as a capacity planning input and as a diagnostic signal: significant spikes in AHT for a specific issue type often indicate a knowledge gap, a broken process, or a system failure that is making agents work around something. As a primary performance target for individual agents, it is counterproductive: agents who are incentivised to reduce AHT resolve fewer issues on first contact, generating re-contacts that cost more in total than the handle time saved.

Watch for: Don't set individual AHT targets without pairing them with FCR and quality scores. AHT without FCR is a false economy.

3. Occupancy Rate

Target: 80–85%

The proportion of time agents spend handling contacts or in after-call work, compared to total time logged in. Occupancy is the primary agent utilisation metric. Below 80% and your staffing cost per contact is too high: agents are idle a significant proportion of their paid time. Above 85 to 90% and agents have no recovery time between contacts, which raises error rates, lowers quality scores, and accelerates attrition. At 90%+ occupancy, attrition typically increases within 6 to 12 months, creating a recruitment and training cost that far exceeds any efficiency gain from high utilisation.

Watch for: Occupancy above 88% sustained over more than a quarter is a leading indicator of attrition. Treat it as a staffing warning signal, not an efficiency target to maintain.

4. Abandonment Rate

Target: under 5%

The percentage of inbound contacts that disconnect before reaching an agent. Abandonment is primarily a staffing and routing signal: customers are leaving the queue because wait times are too long, IVR paths are too complex, or digital containment is failing. An abandonment rate above 5% in most sectors indicates a structural capacity or routing problem. In financial services, sustained high abandonment can be a regulatory concern if customers are abandoning during time-sensitive interactions (payment disputes, fraud reports, account access issues).

Watch for: Abandonment rate varies significantly by time of day and day of week. An average of 4% may mask a 15% rate during peak hours, which is the number that actually affects customer experience.

5. Customer Satisfaction Score (CSAT) and Customer Effort Score (CES)

CSAT: 80%+ | CES: 5.5+ / 7

CSAT measures how satisfied a customer was with an interaction (typically 1–5 scale, reported as % rating 4 or 5). CES measures how easy it was to get the issue resolved (1–7 scale). Both are post-interaction survey metrics. CES is increasingly preferred in contact centres because Gartner research found that reducing customer effort is a stronger predictor of loyalty than satisfaction alone: a customer who rated an interaction as satisfying but difficult to navigate is still a churn risk. For regulated industries with complex interactions, CES tends to surface friction that CSAT masks.

Watch for: Survey response rates below 10% make both metrics statistically unreliable. Use behavioural signals (repeat contacts, complaint rate) as a cross-check rather than relying solely on survey data.

6. Cost Per Contact

Benchmark: £4–£8 (voice), £1–£3 (digital)

Total contact centre operating cost divided by total contacts handled. Cost per contact is the primary efficiency metric for leadership and finance. It is most useful when tracked by channel: voice contacts are typically 3 to 5 times more expensive than digital contacts (chat, email, WhatsApp), which is why channel mix matters so much to overall productivity. A contact centre that shifts 20% of volume from voice to digital channels can reduce cost per contact significantly without touching agent headcount, service level agreements, or quality scores.

Watch for: Cost per contact calculations often exclude technology costs and training. Include the full cost base or the metric will consistently underestimate the true per-contact cost and overstate efficiency gains.

7. Schedule Adherence

Target: 90–95%

The percentage of time agents are available to handle contacts during their scheduled hours. Schedule adherence is the link between workforce planning and actual capacity. A workforce management model that forecasts 50 agents available during peak hours is only accurate if those 50 agents are actually logged in and available during those hours. Adherence below 90% typically means queue build-up during predictable peak periods despite technically adequate staffing levels. It is often a scheduling design problem rather than a behaviour problem: rigid schedules that don't account for natural variation in break timing, wrap-up time, or coaching sessions tend to produce low adherence.

Watch for: Adherence targets above 95% tend to create perverse behaviour (agents skipping breaks, rushing after-call work) that increases errors and accelerates fatigue.

8. Agent Utilisation Rate

Target: 75–80% of total paid hours

Distinct from occupancy, agent utilisation measures productive time (handling contacts plus after-call work plus training plus coaching) as a proportion of total paid hours including breaks, meetings, and shrinkage. It is the most comprehensive view of how efficiently paid agent hours translate into productive output. A utilisation rate below 70% indicates either overstaffing or significant non-productive time (excessive meeting load, long briefings, system downtime). Above 85% and shrinkage allowances are likely being compressed in ways that will surface as quality or attrition problems.

Watch for: Utilisation and occupancy are related but not the same metric. Occupancy only counts time logged in; utilisation accounts for all paid hours including scheduled non-contact time.

How these metrics interact and conflict

The most common productivity mistakes come from optimising one metric without accounting for its effect on the others.

Optimising AHT without FCR is a false economy. Shorter calls that don't resolve generate re-contacts that cost more in total than the time saved.

The key interactions to manage:

  • FCR and AHT: Improving FCR often increases AHT slightly on the first contact. The net effect on total volume is almost always positive: more time per resolved contact, but fewer total contacts. The maths favours resolution over speed.
  • Occupancy and attrition: High occupancy reduces cost per contact in the short term. Sustained high occupancy increases attrition, which increases recruitment and training cost. The crossover point: where attrition cost exceeds efficiency gain: is typically around 88 to 90% occupancy over a sustained period.
  • AHT and CSAT: AHT reduction initiatives that cut interaction quality consistently reduce CSAT within 2 to 3 months. CSAT decline predicts increased contact volume as dissatisfied customers call back or escalate.
  • Schedule adherence and FCR: Poor adherence creates queue build-up during peak hours. Agents handling contacts in a pressured queue resolve less effectively than agents with adequate capacity. Adherence is therefore an upstream FCR lever: fixing it improves FCR without touching agent capability or process.

The metrics that feel useful but aren't

Some metrics are widely tracked in contact centres but have limited operational value as primary productivity indicators:

  • Total contacts handled: Volume without context tells you nothing about productivity. A contact centre that handles 10,000 contacts per day with a 60% FCR is performing worse than one handling 7,000 with an 80% FCR.
  • Average speed of answer (ASA): Useful as a queue health signal but a poor proxy for quality. A contact answered in 10 seconds and transferred twice is worse than one answered in 90 seconds and resolved immediately.
  • Net Promoter Score (NPS) from post-call surveys: NPS measures brand loyalty, not interaction quality. It is influenced by product experience, pricing, and factors entirely outside the contact centre's control. Using NPS as a contact centre performance metric conflates channel quality with brand perception.
  • Talk time as a proportion of AHT: Unless after-call work is disproportionately high for a specific process, the talk/wrap split within AHT is rarely a useful optimisation target.

How technology moves the dial on productivity metrics

The metrics described above are not fixed by the nature of the work. Technology choices directly influence where each metric settles.

Routing and IVR: Skills-based routing that correctly identifies intent and matches to agent capability improves FCR by 6 to 10 percentage points in most deployments. It also reduces AHT for matched contacts because the right agent resolves faster than a general agent. Intelligent IVR design is the highest-ROI routing investment in most contact centres.

Unified customer context: Giving every agent a single timeline of all prior interactions: across voice, chat, email, SMS, and WhatsApp: eliminates the re-explanation cycle that extends AHT and reduces FCR. Agents with full context resolve faster and more accurately. Our omnichannel platform is built to surface this view at the agent level, integrated with the CRM in real time.

Proactive outbound messaging: A significant proportion of inbound contacts are predictable re-contacts: customers chasing an update they expected but did not receive. Automated transactional messages: triggered when a back-office action completes, a delivery status changes, or a case is updated: eliminate these contacts before they arrive. Each prevented inbound contact improves every efficiency metric simultaneously: FCR, cost per contact, occupancy, and abandonment rate all benefit from lower unnecessary volume.

Digital channel containment: Shifting contacts from voice to chat, WhatsApp, or SMS reduces cost per contact by 60 to 75% per interaction. Done well, digital containment also improves CES because customers can interact asynchronously at their own pace rather than waiting on hold. Done poorly: with chatbots that can't resolve anything: it increases effort and drives customers back to voice with greater frustration.

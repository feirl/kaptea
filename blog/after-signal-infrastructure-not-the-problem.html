After Twilio Signal: Why the Infrastructure Was Never the Problem

14 May 2026 · 5 min read · By Alan McNab, CEO & Co-Founder

The infrastructure for AI-driven customer engagement is now production-ready. What has not been solved is the design problem: who has thought carefully about what a kept promise actually looks like for each customer, across every channel, at scale.

  2. What we have been building toward

I was at Twilio's Signal conference last week. And I want to be direct about what I saw: the infrastructure layer for AI-driven customer engagement has now been built. It is production-ready, it is generally available, and it is genuinely impressive.

Twilio described it as the largest product release in the company's history. Conversation Memory that persists across every channel and every interaction. A Conversation Orchestrator that treats the conversation, not the session or the ticket, as the system of record. A real-time Conversation Intelligence layer that listens, scores, and surfaces signals as a conversation is happening. And Agent Connect, an abstraction layer that lets any AI agent from any provider plug into all of it.

"Intelligence without context is guesswork, and intelligence without orchestration is noise."

That was Twilio CEO Khozema Shipchandler at Signal. It is a precise diagnosis of what most AI implementations in customer engagement are today: fast, capable, but fundamentally disconnected from what the customer actually needs.

So the infrastructure problem has been solved. What has not been solved is the design problem.

The efficiency trap

Most AI deployments in customer engagement have been built around a single objective: contain the interaction. Route the customer away from a human. Answer the most common questions. Reduce handle time. The KPIs are efficiency metrics: containment rate, automation rate, cost per contact.

These are not wrong objectives. But they produce a specific class of failure: the experience where something went wrong and the system had no architecture for what happened next. The AI said it was resolved. The customer knew it was not. Nobody closed the loop. The next interaction started from zero.

This failure is not a technology failure. It is a design failure. The system was never asked what a kept promise looks like for this customer. It was never designed to carry forward the knowledge that the last three interactions on this issue were unsatisfactory. It was never built to ensure satisfaction, only to reach a status of "resolved."

And with the infrastructure Twilio has now made available, those design decisions are no longer constrained by what the platform can do. The platform can do all of it. The question is whether someone has thought carefully about what "resolved" actually means.

What we have been building toward

At Kaptea, we have spent years working with a framework developed by Fernando Flores, a philosopher and organisational theorist who, forty-five years ago, identified something that turns out to be more relevant today than when he wrote it.

His insight was this: every meaningful interaction between a customer and a business is a promise cycle. The customer makes a request. The business shapes a response, a commitment to do something specific. That commitment is either kept or it is not. If it is not kept, and nothing happens to acknowledge and repair that, trust erodes. Not slowly. Structurally.

What Flores understood, and what the Harvard Business Review validated in their research on Promise-Based Management in 2007, is that most execution failures in organisations are not resource failures or technology failures. They are commitment failures. Promises made without the context to keep them. Promises technically "closed" but never actually fulfilled in the customer's experience.

We call our application of this framework the Loop. Four quadrants: Listening, Co-Design, Getting It Done, and Ensuring Satisfaction. Each one corresponds to a stage in the promise cycle. Each one now has a direct technical counterpart in what Twilio launched at Signal.

This is the foundation of how we approach customer journey orchestration: not as a routing problem, but as a trust architecture problem.

The three layers

Here is how I now think about the architecture of AI-driven customer engagement:

Layer 1: The infrastructure

Conversational primitives: persistent memory across every channel and interaction, an orchestration spine that treats the conversation as the system of record, real-time intelligence, and an abstraction layer for any AI agent from any provider. World-class, production-ready, and deliberately neutral.

Layer 2: The orchestration operating system

What our team has built inside Pronto. Multi-agent routing. Version-controlled AI deployments. Runtime snapshots and rollback. Shared memory continuity across every agent and every handoff. The layer that makes the infrastructure manageable, observable, and enterprise-grade.

Layer 3: The methodology

The Flores Loop. This is the layer that determines how the orchestrations behave: what signals the system listens for, when an AI agent escalates to a human, what "resolved" means for this customer in this context, how trust is built rather than assumed.

Most vendors competing in this space are selling Layer 1 or Layer 2. Nobody else is explicitly designing Layer 3.

What Twilio left blank on purpose

At Signal, a Twilio executive said directly: "We are not here to prescribe a model, framework, or data strategy."

That is not modesty. That is their business model. Twilio serves everyone from startups to the world's largest enterprises. They cannot and will not prescribe how the orchestration layer should behave. They will build the best possible infrastructure and leave the design methodology open.

Every other Twilio partner will configure these tools for efficiency. Faster routing. Higher containment. Lower handle time. That is a legitimate use of the infrastructure. It will produce better chatbots.

Kaptea configures them for trust. The Language Operators we design are not general-purpose: they are built to detect whether a promise made earlier in the conversation is at risk, whether a customer's confidence is wavering, or whether an interaction is heading toward the same failure it has produced before. The handoff conditions we design are grounded in what a kept promise looks like, not just a threshold metric. The post-interaction workflows we build close the loop genuinely, not performatively.

Why this matters now

44% of enterprise AI leaders have only moderate confidence that AI agents can act autonomously without human intervention. That is not a technology readiness problem. The technology is ready. It is a trust architecture problem. Nobody has designed the layer that makes AI in customer engagement trustworthy.

The infrastructure is ready. The orchestration is ready. The question is whether the design intelligence has been thought through carefully enough to make it work: not just quickly, but reliably, and in a way that builds trust with each interaction rather than eroding it.

That is the problem we are built to solve. And for the first time, the infrastructure layer is good enough to solve it at scale.

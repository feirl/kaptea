Conversational AI in Contact Centres: What Actually Works in 2026

23 June 2026 · 7 min read · Alan McNab

Every contact centre conversation about AI starts with a vendor telling you they can deflect 80 per cent of your calls. Then you ask for a reference site that has actually achieved that. The silence is instructive.

Quick answer

Conversational AI in contact centres is the use of natural language processing to handle or assist with customer interactions across voice, chat, and messaging channels: either resolving queries end-to-end without human involvement, or surfacing information and suggestions to live agents in real time. The realistic deflection rate for a well-implemented conversational AI deployment handling transactional queries is 25 to 45 per cent. Claims of 70 to 80 per cent deflection typically reflect cherry-picked query types or short measurement windows before callers learn to bypass the bot.

The gap between vendor positioning and operational reality in contact centre AI is wider than in almost any other enterprise technology category. That is partly because the technology genuinely works, in the right contexts, and vendors have cherry-picked those contexts to build their case studies. It is also because deflection is easy to measure and hard to define: a bot that ends a conversation without escalating it has "deflected" that contact regardless of whether the customer actually got an answer.

This article is an attempt to be precise. What the technology actually does, where the numbers come from, what it consistently fails at, and what a compliant deployment looks like in a regulated environment.

What conversational AI is actually doing in contact centres today

Conversational AI in a contact centre takes three distinct forms, and they have very different ROI profiles. Conflating them is the source of most of the confusion in the market.

Full automation: the AI resolves the query end-to-end

This is the model most vendors lead with. A customer contacts the centre via voice, webchat, or messaging. A virtual agent, powered by natural language understanding, identifies the customer's intent, retrieves the relevant information or completes the transaction, and closes the interaction without any human involvement. No agent is ever involved.

Full automation works well for a narrow set of queries: those that are genuinely transactional, where the intent is unambiguous, the required action is low-risk, and the necessary data integrations are in place. Account balance checks, order status, appointment bookings, standard FAQs. When those conditions are met, the economics are compelling: cost per automated contact is typically a fraction of the cost per agent-handled contact.

The mistake is assuming those conditions apply broadly. In regulated sectors, a large proportion of inbound contacts are not clean, transactional, and low-risk. They involve accounts with flags, partial information, or situations that require judgment. The AI starts the interaction well and then encounters something slightly non-standard, at which point the experience deteriorates rapidly.

Agent assist: AI augments every live interaction

Agent assist is, in most deployments, where the more reliable and often larger ROI lives. The AI listens to the live conversation in real time, retrieves relevant knowledge articles, suggests responses, flags compliance risks, and automatically completes post-call wrap-up tasks including call summarisation and CRM updates. The agent is still handling the interaction; the AI is reducing the cognitive load and administrative overhead of doing so.

The critical difference from full automation is scope. Agent assist applies to every contact the centre handles, not just the subset that is suitable for automation. If a contact centre handles 100,000 calls per month and 30 per cent are suitable for full automation, agent assist applies to the other 70,000 as well. Shaving 45 seconds off average handle time across all contacts compounds quickly. See our analysis of key contact centre productivity metrics for the arithmetic on that.

Intelligent routing: AI classifies intent before the interaction reaches an agent

The third model, and frequently the best entry point for organisations that are new to contact centre AI, is using conversational AI for intent classification and structured routing. The AI identifies what the customer wants, their sentiment, any relevant account context, and routes them to the right team with that context already surfaced on the agent desktop. No cold transfers. No "can you tell me what this is regarding" at the start of every call.

This model requires less integration depth than full automation and generates measurable improvements in first-contact resolution almost immediately, because agents start with context rather than having to build it. It also creates the data foundation: structured intent data and sentiment data, for deciding which queries to automate next.

The deflection rate reality check

Vendor deflection claims of 70 to 80 per cent are almost never achieved in sustained, production deployments handling a realistic cross-section of contact types. There are three specific reasons why.

Cherry-picked query scope. A vendor case study that achieves 75 per cent deflection has typically scoped the deployment to a narrow set of transactional intents where the technology performs well. The total volume of contacts that fall outside that scope, and are still handled by agents, is not included in the deflection calculation.

Short measurement windows. Deflection rates at month one are almost always higher than deflection rates at month six. Customers who encounter the bot early, find it unsatisfactory, and learn to say "speak to an agent" or dial back and press zero will do so. The cohort of contacts that reach the bot changes over time as customers route around it.

Deflection versus resolution. The most important distinction is between a contact that the bot "handled" and a contact that was actually resolved. A bot that ends a conversation by saying "I've noted your query, a team member will be in touch" has deflected the contact from an agent in that moment, but the underlying query still needs resolution. If you measure resolution at seven days rather than at point of contact, the picture looks materially different.

The table below reflects realistic performance ranges based on deployed systems handling a representative cross-section of contacts in each query category.

Query type Realistic AI deflection Notes
Account balance / order status / appointment booking 55–70% High confidence, narrow intent, fully transactional. Requires back-end system integration.
Password reset / access issue 60–75% Mostly automatable where identity verification and system integrations are in place.
General FAQ 30–50% Depends entirely on FAQ quality, scope, and how recently the knowledge base has been updated.
Product advice / recommendation 10–20% High compliance risk in regulated sectors. AI cannot give regulated advice.

Where AI consistently underperforms

The categories where conversational AI regularly fails are not a function of immaturity in the technology. They are structural. Understanding them is more useful than hoping the next model release will solve them.

Complex emotional interactions. Complaints, bereavements, financial hardship, and vulnerable customer situations are not suitable for AI-first handling. Customers in genuine distress do not want to be handled by a bot. They want to be heard by a human. An AI that correctly identifies a hardship query and routes it to a specialist is doing its job well. An AI that attempts to resolve it autonomously is creating both a poor customer experience and a regulatory risk.

Queries requiring professional judgment. In financial services, insurance, and healthcare, a large category of customer queries requires regulated advice or clinical judgment. Large language models can generate plausible-sounding responses to these queries. They cannot provide regulated advice. Any deployment that allows AI to answer questions that require a regulated individual to answer them is a compliance failure waiting to happen.

Multi-step processes with exception handling. The conversational AI starts the interaction well, correctly identifies intent, and begins the resolution flow. Then the customer's situation turns out to be slightly non-standard: a joint account, an address that does not match records, a product that was purchased under a promotional rate that has expired. The AI encounters an exception its design did not anticipate, and the interaction deteriorates. These failures are hard to design out entirely, but they are why measuring resolution at seven days rather than at point of contact matters.

First-contact resolution on complex issues. Contact centre AI frequently scores well on deflection KPIs and poorly on FCR when measured properly. A bot that deflects a contact but does not actually resolve the underlying issue has transferred cost rather than eliminated it: the customer will call back, and the second interaction will often be harder to handle. This is the single most important reason to design voice and IVR journeys carefully rather than treating deflection as the primary success metric.

Consumer Duty and what it means for AI deployment

Since July 2023, the FCA's Consumer Duty has changed the compliance context for any customer-facing interaction in a regulated firm. The core requirement is that firms must be able to evidence that customers are receiving good outcomes. That requirement applies to AI-handled interactions in exactly the same way it applies to agent-handled ones.

In practice, this creates four specific operational requirements for any firm deploying conversational AI in a regulated contact centre.

Full conversation logging. Every AI-handled interaction needs to be captured, stored, and retrievable. This is not just good practice: it is a prerequisite for evidencing outcomes under the Duty. A bot interaction that is not logged is an interaction you cannot defend if challenged.

Outcome measurement. Deflection is not an outcome. Resolution is an outcome. Firms need to measure whether AI-handled contacts result in queries actually being answered, not just interactions being terminated. This requires follow-up measurement: call-back rates, repeat contacts on the same issue, complaint rates attributable to AI-handled interactions.

Escalation monitoring. The point at which a customer tries to escalate from the AI to a human, and whether that escalation is handled successfully, is a critical outcome measure. Blocking escalation, intentionally or by design, is a Consumer Duty concern. Systems need to make escalation easy and log every instance of it.

Human oversight of AI decision pathways. The FCA's 2022 discussion paper on AI explicitly flagged explainability and audit trails as areas of increasing scrutiny. Firms need to be able to explain why the AI gave the response it gave, which requires logging the intent classification, the knowledge retrieval, and the response generation at a level of granularity that supports audit. Our control and governance framework covers how to structure this in practice.

None of this means you cannot use conversational AI in a regulated contact centre. It means you need to design for auditability from the start rather than retrofitting it after deployment.

How to build the business case

The most common mistake in building the business case for contact centre AI is starting with deflection rate. Start instead with cost per contact and the realistic proportion of your contacts that are suitable for automation.

A straightforward model: if your contact centre handles 50,000 contacts per month at an average cost of £5 per contact, and 25 per cent of those contacts are genuinely transactional, that is 12,500 automatable contacts per month. If conversational AI can deflect 60 per cent of those at a cost of £0.25 per automated interaction, the monthly saving is approximately £28,000 before implementation and overhead costs. That is a reasonable and achievable projection. A headline figure suggesting 70 per cent deflection of all 50,000 contacts would require the technology to handle contact types it consistently cannot handle well, and the projection will not survive contact with your actual contact mix.

The agent assist ROI is often larger in aggregate and easier to achieve. If average handle time across all contacts is four minutes, and agent assist reduces that by 30 seconds through real-time knowledge retrieval and automated wrap-up, that is a 12.5 per cent reduction in AHT applied to all 50,000 contacts, not just the automatable ones. That typically exceeds the full automation saving while also improving agent experience and reducing training time for new hires.

Start with intelligent routing and agent assist. Build the data foundation. Then design the automation layer for the specific contact types where the numbers justify it and the regulatory risk is manageable. That sequence is less exciting than an 80 per cent deflection headline, but it is the sequence that actually delivers.

Frequently asked questions

What is conversational AI in a contact centre?

Conversational AI in a contact centre is software that uses natural language understanding (NLU) to interpret customer queries and respond in natural language, rather than through rigid menu trees or keyword matching. Unlike a basic chatbot that matches phrases to canned responses, conversational AI understands intent, handles variations in phrasing, maintains context across multiple turns of a conversation, and can integrate with back-end systems to complete transactions. In a contact centre, it is typically deployed as a virtual agent on voice, webchat, WhatsApp, or SMS, either as a first-line deflection layer or as a tool that assists live agents with suggested responses and real-time knowledge retrieval.

What deflection rate can you realistically expect from conversational AI?

For a well-implemented system handling transactional queries (account balance, order status, appointment booking, FAQs), a realistic deflection rate is 25 to 45 per cent across the full contact mix. Vendors commonly quote 70 to 80 per cent, but those figures typically reflect controlled conditions, narrow query scopes, or measurement windows before customers learn the system's limitations and route around it. The best predictor of sustainable deflection is the proportion of inbound contacts that are genuinely transactional and low-risk to automate. In regulated environments like financial services and healthcare, this proportion is often 20 to 30 per cent of total volume, which sets a practical ceiling on autonomous resolution.

What is the difference between a chatbot and conversational AI?

A chatbot typically follows a decision tree: it matches keywords or phrases to pre-written responses and routes the conversation through a fixed flow. Conversational AI uses machine learning models trained on natural language to understand intent, handle ambiguity, and generate contextually appropriate responses. The practical difference is that a chatbot fails whenever a customer says something outside its script, while conversational AI degrades more gracefully and can handle variations in how the same question is asked. Modern deployments usually combine both: NLU at the front end to classify intent, and structured flows to handle the resolution steps once intent is known.

Does the FCA have rules on using AI in customer-facing contact centres?

The FCA does not have a specific rule on conversational AI, but several existing obligations apply. Under Consumer Duty (effective July 2023), firms must ensure customers receive outcomes that are fair and can evidence this: automated interactions are not exempt. COBS 2.1 requires communications to be clear, fair, and not misleading: a conversational AI that gives incorrect information about a financial product is a regulatory risk. The FCA's 2022 AI discussion paper signalled increased scrutiny of explainability and audit trails. In practice, regulated firms using AI in customer interactions need to log all AI-handled conversations, monitor for errors and harm, and maintain human oversight of AI decision pathways.

CPaaS Security Certifications for UK Regulated Industries: What to Look For

Robert Norton · 7 July 2026 · 7 min read

Every enterprise procurement process for a CPaaS provider now includes a security questionnaire. Most are long. Very few are specific enough to catch the gaps that matter for regulated industries. The standard ISO checkbox is already ticked by everyone on the shortlist. The real work starts after that.

This post cuts through to the six criteria that actually differentiate vendors when you move past the baseline security layer into the compliance-specific requirements that FCA-regulated firms, NHS trusts, and utilities operators face. It also covers the dedicated versus shared instance question, which is where compliance gaps tend to surface first after an incident or a regulatory review.

Quick answer

When choosing a CPaaS (Communications Platform as a Service) provider for UK regulated industries, the certifications that matter most are ISO 27001 (information security management), SOC 2 Type II (operational security controls), UK GDPR data residency (data processed and stored within the UK or EEA), WORM-compliant message archiving (required for FCA and MiFID II), and carrier-grade uptime SLAs of 99.95% or above. A vendor holding all five gives regulated procurement teams a defensible basis for supplier approval. Vendors holding only one or two may pass a basic security questionnaire but leave compliance gaps that become visible only after an incident or regulatory review.

Why standard security due diligence misses regulated-industry requirements

Most procurement security checklists were designed for SaaS software, not communications infrastructure. They ask about penetration testing schedules, SOC 2 certification, and data encryption standards. All CPaaS vendors of any scale pass those questions. The problem is category mismatch: the checklist is asking the right questions for a project management tool or HR platform, but the wrong questions for a communications layer that handles millions of regulated messages per month.

What those checklists miss is the compliance-specific layer that sits on top of standard security controls. WORM archiving is not a security feature; it is a regulatory requirement for firms operating under MiFID II Article 16, FCA COBS rules, and NHS records retention obligations. Message-level audit logs are not the same as system access logs. UK data residency is not the same as encryption at rest. And the ability to produce a complete, timestamped archive of a specific customer's communications history on demand for an FCA review is a capability that most shared CPaaS deployments simply cannot provide reliably.

The gap is not the result of bad intent by procurement teams or vendors. It is the result of applying a general-purpose security framework to a category that needs a compliance-specific layer on top. The practical fix is to run two checklists in parallel: the standard security questionnaire your procurement team already uses, and a separate compliance questionnaire built specifically for communications infrastructure in regulated industries.

The six criteria that separate compliant CPaaS from security-checked CPaaS

These are the criteria that separate vendors who will pass your annual security review from vendors who will pass your annual security review and your FCA supplier sign-off, your MiFID II audit, and your DPO's periodic data processing review.

1. ISO 27001: scope matters more than the certificate

ISO 27001 is the floor, not the ceiling. Every serious CPaaS vendor holds it. The question that separates vendors is not whether they have the certificate but what is in scope. Some vendors hold ISO 27001 for their head office operations while their communications platform infrastructure runs under a separate certification boundary or no formal ISMS at all.

Ask specifically: is the communications platform itself within the ISO 27001 scope? Which datacentres are in scope? What is excluded from the certification boundary? Request the Statement of Applicability, not just the certificate, and check whether the controls relevant to your use case (access control, cryptography, supplier relationships, incident management) are marked as implemented rather than not applicable.

2. SOC 2 Type II: the report period matters

SOC 2 Type I is a point-in-time assessment: an auditor reviews whether controls are designed correctly at a single moment. SOC 2 Type II verifies that controls are operating effectively over a period of time, typically six to twelve months. For a communications platform processing regulated messages continuously, the distinction is material. A Type I report tells you the controls look right on paper. A Type II report tells you they actually worked in practice across a sustained period.

When evaluating vendors, ask for the Type II report directly and check the report period. A report covering six months ending eighteen months ago provides much weaker assurance than a recent twelve-month report. Some vendors obtain Type II certification once and do not renew it promptly. Ask for the renewal date as well as the certificate.

3. UK and EEA data residency: contractual guarantee versus default setting

Many CPaaS platforms default to routing data through UK or EEA infrastructure. Defaulting is not the same as guaranteeing. The compliance question is whether data residency is contractually committed, whether it covers both primary processing and failover routing, and what happens to your data if the platform experiences a regional outage and fails over to a non-EEA region.

For UK GDPR compliance, personal data cannot be transferred to third countries without an appropriate transfer mechanism. If your CPaaS provider cannot demonstrate that failover does not result in data leaving the UK or EEA, that is a potential UK GDPR Article 44 issue. Ask for this commitment in the contract, not just in the product documentation, and ask specifically about failover behaviour.

4. WORM-compliant archiving: a technical requirement, not a product feature

WORM stands for write-once read-many. In the context of communications archiving, it means that messages cannot be deleted, modified, or overwritten before the regulatory retention period ends. This is a hard technical requirement for firms operating under MiFID II Article 16 (five-year minimum retention for investment services communications), FCA COBS rules, and NHS records retention obligations.

The key question is not whether a vendor offers archiving, but whether the archiving is tamper-evident and non-deletable during the retention period. Many CPaaS platforms offer message logging or export capabilities that do not meet WORM standards. Ask whether archives are stored in immutable storage with deletion prevention policies, whether the retention period is enforced at the storage layer rather than by application logic, and whether the vendor can produce a certificate of destruction or retention compliance report on demand.

5. Carrier-grade uptime SLA of 99.95% or above

A 99.95% uptime SLA translates to a maximum downtime of approximately 4.4 hours per year. That figure matters not just as an operational metric but as a regulatory one. Under FCA Consumer Duty, firms must ensure customers can reach them for time-sensitive matters, including complaints, claims, and account changes. Communications infrastructure downtime that prevents customers from accessing these services can constitute a service failure requiring FCA notification.

The distinction between a marketing claim and a financially-backed SLA is significant. Ask for: the SLA percentage, the financial remedy for SLA breach, historical uptime data for the past twelve months, and whether the SLA covers the full communications path (including carrier connectivity) or only the platform layer. Many providers publish impressive uptime figures for their platform while excluding carrier-related failures that account for most real-world outages.

6. Data Processing Agreement under UK GDPR Article 28

A signed DPA must be in place before a CPaaS provider processes any personal data on your behalf. This is not optional and it is not covered by standard vendor terms of service. UK GDPR Article 28 requires specific commitments from the processor: limiting sub-processors, implementing appropriate technical and organisational measures, supporting subject access requests and deletion rights, and returning or deleting data at contract end.

Some CPaaS providers offer generic DPA templates that do not list sub-processors, do not commit to specific data residency, and do not address communications-specific obligations such as metadata retention. For regulated firms, the DPA should list all sub-processors (including carrier partners and cloud infrastructure providers), commit to notifying you of sub-processor changes, and align retention periods with your regulatory obligations rather than defaulting to vendor retention policies.

The dedicated versus shared instance question

After the six criteria above, the next question that materially affects compliance is whether you are deploying on a dedicated instance or a shared one. This distinction is often presented as a performance question (dedicated instances offer more headroom for volume spikes) but the compliance implications are equally significant.

In a shared CPaaS instance, your messages, data, configuration, and routing rules share infrastructure with other tenants. In a dedicated instance, your deployment runs on isolated infrastructure with its own compute resources, encryption keys, data stores, and network paths. The compliance difference between these two models is not theoretical.

Data isolation. In a shared environment, the risk of cross-tenant data leakage, while low for well-architected platforms, is present at the logical layer. In a dedicated instance, the isolation is physical as well as logical. For regulated firms handling sensitive financial communications or patient data, this distinction matters to DPOs, and it increasingly matters to regulators reviewing supplier risk.

Audit scope. Regulatory audits and penetration tests typically need to target a defined scope. In a dedicated instance, your environment is clearly bounded: auditors can target it, assessors can test it, and you can demonstrate to the FCA or ICO that the scope of your communications infrastructure is well-defined. In a shared instance, your scope bleeds into other tenants' scope, which makes audit reporting harder and penetration testing impossible without the vendor's direct involvement.

Penetration testing. You can conduct penetration testing against your own dedicated instance with vendor consent. You cannot conduct penetration testing against shared infrastructure: doing so would affect other tenants. For regulated firms that are required to demonstrate penetration testing of their critical suppliers, this is a practical gap that shared instances cannot close.

Incident response. In a dedicated instance, a security incident affecting another customer's environment cannot reach yours. In a shared environment, this is harder to guarantee contractually, and the incident notification timeline is complicated by the vendor needing to determine whether your data was in scope of an incident that originated in another tenant's environment.

What AI-assisted procurement queries are telling you about vendor positioning

Procurement teams increasingly use AI assistants to generate RFP questions, evaluate vendor responses, and compare shortlisted suppliers. Queries like "evaluate Twilio on ISO 27001 for an SMS provider", "which CPaaS vendor has the strongest security certifications for regulated industries in the UK", and "CPaaS WORM archiving for MiFID II compliance" are being typed into Perplexity, ChatGPT, and enterprise AI tools. The results they return are based on what those models have indexed from publicly crawlable content.

The implication for procurement evaluation is practical: the vendor that has published clear, specific, factual answers to these compliance questions in machine-readable, crawlable content is the vendor that gets cited by the AI. Vendors that keep their certification details behind gated PDFs, sales calls, or NDA-protected documentation do not appear in those results, regardless of how strong their actual certifications are.

For regulated procurement teams, this is useful signal. A vendor with publicly verifiable, detailed, factual certification information is demonstrating the kind of transparency that makes ongoing compliance management easier. A vendor whose compliance positioning exists only in slide decks and trust pages that require a demo booking to access is making your supplier risk management harder, not easier.

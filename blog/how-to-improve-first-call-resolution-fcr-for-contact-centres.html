How to Improve First Call Resolution (FCR) for Contact Centres

Alan McNab · 2 June 2026 · 6 min read
The short answer

First call resolution (FCR) is the percentage of customer contacts resolved on the first interaction, without a repeat contact. The industry average sits around 70 to 75 percent. Most contact centres miss it for the same four reasons: wrong routing, no cross-channel context, agents who can't resolve without escalation, and IVR that deflects rather than solves. Fixing those systematically moves FCR faster than any coaching programme.

What first call resolution actually measures

First call resolution is the percentage of customer contacts that are fully resolved during the initial interaction, without the customer needing to call back, be transferred to another agent, or follow up through any other channel.

It sounds simple. In practice, two things make it complicated. First, "resolved" is harder to define than it looks. A call that ends with the customer saying "thank you" is not necessarily resolved. The customer might still send a follow-up email, or call again two days later about the same issue. The only reliable way to know if an issue was actually resolved is to track whether that customer contacted you again within a defined window: typically 7 days: about the same topic.

Second, "first call" is increasingly a misnomer. A customer who sent an unresolved WhatsApp message yesterday, then calls today, has already experienced a failure: even if this is technically the "first" voice interaction. The more useful metric for modern contact centres is first contact resolution: whether the issue was resolved on the first interaction, regardless of channel.

For the purposes of this article, FCR covers both: the operational principles that drive resolution on first interaction are the same whether the channel is voice, chat, email, SMS, or WhatsApp.

Regulated industries sit at the lower end because agents operate under stricter constraints: they cannot always give a definitive answer on first contact, some resolutions require back-office processing, and compliance requirements add steps. These are structural, not just operational. But structural baseline does not mean FCR improvement is impossible: even regulated contact centres can typically move FCR by 8 to 12 percentage points with the right operational changes.

Each 1% improvement in FCR typically drives a 1% improvement in customer satisfaction and a measurable reduction in inbound volume.

The four root causes of low FCR

Most FCR problems trace back to one or more of four root causes. The diagnostic step: before any improvement programme: is to identify which of these is dominant in your operation.

1. Wrong routing on the first contact

The customer reaches an agent who does not have the knowledge, system access, or authority to resolve the issue. This leads to a transfer (which is an immediate FCR failure, counted as two contacts) or a callback promise (which is a deferred FCR failure). IVR design and skills-based routing are the main levers here. A customer who calls about a billing dispute and reaches a general service agent will almost always require a transfer or callback.

2. No cross-channel context

The agent has no visibility of what the customer did before the call. If a customer sent a chat message two days ago, submitted a web form yesterday, and is now calling, the agent starts from scratch. This forces the customer to re-explain, extends handle time, and increases the chance of misdiagnosis. It is also a significant source of customer frustration that compounds the original issue.

3. Agents lack the authority or information to resolve

The issue is in the right queue, with the right agent, but the agent cannot resolve it without escalation. This is a common pattern in regulated industries: agents can diagnose, but resolution requires a second tier. The fix is partly process (extending authority thresholds) and partly technology (real-time knowledge bases, CRM integration that surfaces relevant policy during the call).

4. The issue was not fully understood on first contact

The agent resolved what they thought the issue was, but the underlying problem was different or broader. The customer calls back because the first resolution did not address the root cause. This is the hardest failure mode to fix because it requires improving agent diagnosis, not just information access or routing.

How to improve FCR: what actually works

Fix routing before anything else

Skills-based routing: sending each contact to the agent most capable of resolving that specific issue: is consistently the highest-return FCR investment. This requires two things: accurate intent identification at the point of contact (IVR, chatbot, or agent triage) and routing rules that match intent to agent capability, not just agent availability.

Most contact centres route to the agent who has been waiting longest. This optimises queue speed, not resolution. IVR design that correctly identifies intent and routes accordingly consistently outperforms general queue models on FCR by 6 to 10 percentage points in our experience across deployments.

Give every agent the full customer timeline

Agents who can see every prior interaction: across every channel: resolve faster and more accurately. This requires a unified customer record that aggregates voice, chat, email, SMS, and WhatsApp history into a single view, accessible during the live interaction.

The technology exists; the operational failure is usually integration. Contact centres often have a CRM, a chat platform, an email system, and a voice platform that do not share data in real time. Agents switch between four screens and piece together context manually. Closing that integration gap: so the agent sees one timeline, not four systems: is one of the most reliable FCR improvements available. Our omnichannel platform is specifically designed to surface this unified view at the agent level.

Reduce unnecessary escalation paths

Map every escalation trigger in your operation and ask two questions: does this escalation need to happen, and if it does, can it happen within the same interaction rather than as a callback? Many escalations are policy escalations: the agent is not authorised to waive a fee, issue a credit, or make a specific commitment. Some of these can be resolved by extending agent authority within defined parameters. Others require a warm transfer (keeping the customer on the line) rather than a callback.

Warm transfer with context handover: where the receiving agent can see the full conversation before they speak: dramatically improves the customer's experience of escalation even when escalation cannot be eliminated.

Use post-call data to identify repeat contact patterns

Every contact centre has clusters of issues that consistently generate repeat contacts. Identify them by tracking repeat contacts from the same customer within 7 days and tagging them by issue type. The issues with the highest repeat rates are your highest-value FCR targets: not because they are the most common, but because each one represents guaranteed future volume.

Fixing a root cause that generates a 30 percent repeat contact rate on 500 contacts per week eliminates 150 inbound contacts per week without any customer satisfaction investment. It also compounds: the customers who would have called back are now satisfied with a single contact, which improves CSAT scores independently.

Proactive outreach before the repeat contact arrives

Some contact types have a predictable re-contact pattern. A customer who calls about a delayed delivery will call again if the delay extends beyond the resolution you gave them. A customer who called about an account query that requires back-office action will call again if they do not receive confirmation within the expected timeframe.

Proactive transactional messaging: an automated SMS or WhatsApp update when the back-office action is completed, or when a delay extends: eliminates a significant proportion of these predictable re-contacts before they happen. This is not strictly FCR improvement (the first contact still did not resolve the issue immediately), but it removes the re-contact that would otherwise register as an FCR failure.

How to measure FCR accurately

FCR measurement is only as good as its definition. The most common measurement failures:

  • Measuring by call outcome, not customer behaviour: "The agent marked it as resolved" is not FCR. Track re-contacts within 7 days from the same customer about the same issue type.
  • Measuring per-channel rather than cross-channel: A customer who calls after an unresolved chat is counted as a new contact by each channel. Cross-channel FCR measurement requires a unified customer identifier across all channels.
  • Excluding transfers from FCR calculation: Any transfer during a contact is an FCR failure for the receiving agent. Excluding transfers artificially inflates FCR and hides routing problems.
  • Using post-call surveys as the primary measure: Survey-based FCR is useful as a secondary signal but should not replace behavioural measurement. Customers who say their issue was resolved sometimes call back; customers who say it was not resolved sometimes do not.

FCR and average handle time: the trade-off that isn't

The most common objection to FCR improvement programmes is that they increase average handle time. If agents spend longer on each call to resolve fully, AHT goes up, and the queue lengthens.

The maths does not support this concern. A contact centre with a 70 percent FCR rate is handling 30 percent more contacts than necessary. If improving FCR to 80 percent increases average handle time by 10 percent, the net effect is still a reduction in total handled volume. Ten percent longer per call, but 15 percent fewer calls: because the 10 percentage point FCR improvement eliminates 10 percent of all contacts as unnecessary re-contacts.

The more significant operational risk is the opposite: contact centres that optimise AHT at the expense of resolution quality. Shorter calls that do not resolve generate re-contacts that are longer than the original, handled by a different agent with no context, and registered as FCR failures. The total cost is higher than a slightly longer first call would have been.
